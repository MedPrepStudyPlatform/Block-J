A 72-year-old man with advanced Parkinson's disease has been on levodopa/carbidopa for 12 years. He now experiences severe "off" periods lasting several hours each day, during which he is completely immobile and unable to speak. His neurologist prescribes apomorphine as an adjunctive therapy. Which of the following is the most important consideration before initiating apomorphine in this patient?
✔ Answer: C
Apomorphine causes severe nausea and vomiting by stimulating D2 receptors in the chemoreceptor trigger zone. Domperidone (a peripheral dopamine antagonist that does not cross the BBB) is given prophylactically to manage this adverse effect without interfering with central therapeutic effects.
A 29-year-old woman presents to the psychiatry clinic with complaints of severe anxiety and panic attacks occurring 3-4 times per week. She has failed trials of sertraline and venlafaxine due to intolerable side effects including nausea and sexual dysfunction. Her psychiatrist suggests starting buspirone. After four weeks of buspirone therapy at 30 mg daily, the patient reports minimal improvement in her anxiety symptoms. Which of the following best explains the lack of response to buspirone in this patient?
✔ Answer: A
Buspirone has a delayed onset of action (typically 2-4 weeks for initial effects, but full anxiolytic response may require 6-8 weeks). The patient should continue therapy before considering it a treatment failure.
A 45-year-old woman with migraines presents to her neurologist with concerns about medication effectiveness. She currently uses sumatriptan 100 mg orally at the onset of migraine headaches. She reports that the medication takes nearly two hours to provide relief, and she often experiences recurrence of headache within 12 hours. Which of the following modifications would most likely provide faster onset of action and longer duration of effect?
✔ Answer: C
Subcutaneous sumatriptan has the fastest onset (10-15 minutes) and highest bioavailability (96%) compared to oral formulations (14% bioavailability, 1-2 hour onset). It is preferred for rapid relief of acute migraine.
Valproate (valproic acid) is a broad-spectrum antiepileptic drug effective against generalized tonic-clonic seizures, absence seizures, and myoclonic seizures. It is also used for migraine prophylaxis and bipolar disorder. Which of the following combination of mechanisms of action is most accurately attributed to valproate?
✔ Answer: C
Valproate has multiple mechanisms including increased GABA synthesis (via GAD activation), inhibition of GABA transaminase, and use-dependent blockade of voltage-gated sodium channels, contributing to its broad antiseizure activity.
Ketamine is an intravenous anesthetic agent that produces dissociative anesthesia characterized by analgesia, amnesia, and catalepsy with preserved airway reflexes and cardiovascular stimulation. Unlike other general anesthetics, ketamine is unique in that it does not cause significant respiratory depression. Which of the following receptor mechanisms is primarily responsible for ketamine's anesthetic and analgesic effects?
✔ Answer: B
Ketamine produces dissociative anesthesia through non-competitive antagonism of NMDA receptors (blocking the ion channel pore), reducing glutamate-mediated excitation in thalamocortical pathways.
Methylxanthines such as theophylline, caffeine, and theobromine are CNS stimulants that produce their effects through multiple mechanisms. In addition to their well-known adenosine receptor antagonism, which of the following additional pharmacological actions contributes to their therapeutic effects in respiratory conditions such as asthma and COPD?
✔ Answer: B
Methylxanthines non-selectively inhibit phosphodiesterase isoenzymes (PDE3, PDE4, PDE5), increasing intracellular cAMP and cGMP, leading to bronchodilation, anti-inflammatory effects, and respiratory muscle stimulation.
A 38-year-old man with schizophrenia has been stable on risperidone 4 mg daily for two years. He presents to the emergency department with a one-day history of fever (39.2°C), generalized muscle rigidity, confusion, and diaphoresis. His heart rate is 140 bpm and blood pressure is 85/50 mmHg. His creatine kinase level is 12000 U/L. Which of the following is the most appropriate immediate pharmacological intervention?
✔ Answer: B
This patient presents with neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS). The first step is immediate discontinuation of the offending antipsychotic (risperidone). Dantrolene (muscle relaxant) and bromocriptine (dopamine agonist) are used for severe cases with significant rigidity and hyperthermia.
A 55-year-old woman presents to her primary care physician with complaints of persistent sadness, anhedonia, fatigue, and poor concentration for the past 6 months. She has no prior history of psychiatric treatment. Her physician diagnoses major depressive disorder and prescribes fluoxetine 20 mg daily. After 5 days of treatment, the patient calls her physician reporting increased anxiety, difficulty sleeping, and feeling "worse than before starting the medication." Which of the following best explains this worsening of symptoms?
✔ Answer: B
SSRI-induced jitteriness/activation syndrome occurs within the first 1-2 weeks of treatment, characterized by anxiety, agitation, insomnia, and feeling worse. It is usually transient and managed with dose reduction or slow titration.
General anesthetics produce reversible loss of consciousness, amnesia, analgesia, and immobility. While the exact mechanisms are complex, most intravenous and inhaled anesthetics enhance inhibitory neurotransmission or reduce excitatory neurotransmission. Which of the following anesthetics primarily exerts its effects through antagonism of NMDA receptors rather than potentiation of GABA-A receptors?
✔ Answer: D
Ketamine is unique among IV anesthetics as it produces dissociative anesthesia primarily through non-competitive NMDA receptor antagonism, while propofol, thiopental, etomidate, and midazolam act via GABA-A receptor potentiation.
Local anesthetics such as lidocaine and bupivacaine produce reversible conduction blockade in peripheral nerves by binding to and inhibiting voltage-gated sodium channels. The potency, duration of action, and toxicity of local anesthetics are influenced by their physicochemical properties. Which of the following properties is primarily responsible for the longer duration of action of bupivacaine compared to lidocaine?
✔ Answer: B
Bupivacaine has higher protein binding (95%) compared to lidocaine (65%), which prolongs its duration of action by retaining the drug at the site of action and reducing systemic absorption.
A 63-year-old man with a history of alcohol use disorder is brought to the emergency department by his family due to confusion, disorientation, and unsteady gait. On examination, he has bilateral nystagmus on horizontal gaze, ophthalmoplegia with restricted eye movements, and inability to stand without support. His family reports that he has been drinking heavily and eating poorly for several months. The physician immediately administers intravenous thiamine. Which additional medication should be avoided before thiamine administration to prevent precipitation of neurological deterioration?
✔ Answer: C
In suspected Wernicke's encephalopathy, glucose administration before thiamine can precipitate or worsen neurological symptoms because glucose metabolism consumes the already depleted thiamine stores, exacerbating metabolic acidosis and neuronal injury.
A 26-year-old woman with a history of generalized tonic-clonic seizures has been maintained on phenytoin 300 mg daily for 5 years with good seizure control. She presents to the neurology clinic with complaints of progressive difficulty walking, gum swelling, and facial coarsening over the past 2 years. On examination, she has gingival hyperplasia and ataxic gait. Her serum phenytoin level is 18 mcg/mL (therapeutic range 10-20). Which of the following best explains her neurological symptoms?
✔ Answer: D
Although the phenytoin level is within therapeutic range, some patients are sensitive to its cerebellar effects. Ataxia, nystagmus, and incoordination are dose-related neurological adverse effects of phenytoin, even at levels at the upper end of therapeutic range.
A 22-year-old man with schizophrenia presents to the emergency department with a one-day history of fever (38.5°C), stiff neck, and severe muscle rigidity. He was started on haloperidol decanoate 100 mg intramuscularly three weeks ago. His blood work shows leukocytosis (WBC 18,000/mcL) and creatine kinase of 350 U/L. Lumbar puncture is normal. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis and what is the appropriate management?
✔ Answer: A
This patient has neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS) presenting with fever, rigidity, and autonomic instability. Normal CSF rules out meningitis. Management includes discontinuing the offending antipsychotic, supportive care, and dantrolene/bromocriptine for severe cases.
The dopamine hypothesis of schizophrenia proposes that hyperactivity of dopaminergic neurotransmission in the mesolimbic pathway contributes to positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions), while hypodopaminergic activity in the mesocortical pathway contributes to negative symptoms (blunted affect, social withdrawal) and cognitive deficits. Which of the following atypical antipsychotics has the highest affinity for serotonin 5-HT2A receptors relative to dopamine D2 receptors, making it particularly effective for negative symptoms with low extrapyramidal side effect risk?
✔ Answer: B
Clozapine has the highest 5-HT2A/D2 ratio among atypical antipsychotics, contributing to its superior efficacy in treatment-resistant schizophrenia and low risk of extrapyramidal side effects, though it requires monitoring for agranulocytosis.
Morphine is a prototype opioid analgesic that produces its effects primarily through agonism at mu-opioid receptors. However, morphine has a unique pharmacokinetic property compared to other opioids such as codeine, oxycodone, and hydromorphone. Which of the following best describes this distinctive characteristic of morphine?
✔ Answer: B
Morphine is glucuronidated to morphine-3-glucuronide (inactive) and morphine-6-glucuronide (active). M6G crosses the blood-brain barrier and is a potent mu-opioid agonist, contributing significantly to morphine's analgesic effect, especially in renal impairment.
Zolpidem is a non-benzodiazepine hypnotic agent approved for the short-term treatment of insomnia characterized by difficulty with sleep initiation. Unlike traditional benzodiazepines, zolpidem exhibits relative selectivity for which specific GABA-A receptor subunit, conferring primarily sedative-hypnotic effects with minimal anxiolytic, myorelaxant, or anticonvulsant activity?
✔ Answer: A
Zolpidem has high selectivity for GABA-A receptors containing the alpha-1 subunit, which are predominantly located in the sleep-promoting regions of the brain, producing sedative effects with reduced muscle relaxant and anxiolytic properties.
A 35-year-old man with a history of heroin use disorder presents to a substance abuse treatment facility seeking medication-assisted treatment. He reports using intravenous heroin daily for the past 3 years and has failed multiple detoxification attempts. His urine toxicology is positive for opioids. The physician recommends starting buprenorphine/naloxone. Which of the following best explains the rationale for including naloxone in this fixed-dose combination product?
✔ Answer: C
In the buprenorphine/naloxone combination (Suboxone), naloxone has poor sublingual bioavailability but precipitates withdrawal if the product is dissolved and injected, serving as an abuse-deterrent formulation.
An 8-year-old boy with a history of absence epilepsy (petit mal) is brought to the neurology clinic by his mother. She reports that he has frequent staring spells lasting 5-10 seconds, during which he is unresponsive and then abruptly returns to normal activity. These episodes occur 20-30 times daily and are interfering with his school performance. He has no history of generalized tonic-clonic seizures. Which of the following is the most appropriate first-line monotherapy for this patient's seizure type?
✔ Answer: D
Ethosuximide is the first-line agent for pure absence seizures. It selectively blocks T-type calcium channels in thalamic relay neurons, which are responsible for generating the spike-and-wave discharges characteristic of absence epilepsy.
Tramadol is a centrally acting analgesic with a unique dual mechanism of action. Unlike typical opioids, tramadol produces analgesia through two distinct pathways. Which of the following correctly describes the dual mechanism of tramadol?
✔ Answer: B
Tramadol and its active metabolite (O-desmethyltramadol) are weak mu-opioid agonists. Additionally, tramadol inhibits norepinephrine and serotonin reuptake (SNRI effect), contributing to its analgesic activity in neuropathic pain.
Selegiline is a monoamine oxidase inhibitor (MAOI) used in the treatment of Parkinson's disease, often as an adjunct to levodopa/carbidopa in patients with motor fluctuations. Unlike non-selective MAOIs (phenelzine, tranylcypromine), selegiline at standard doses does not require dietary tyramine restriction. Which of the following best explains this safety advantage?
✔ Answer: B
At standard doses (5-10 mg daily), selegiline is a selective irreversible inhibitor of MAO-B. MAO-A in the gut remains functional and can metabolize dietary tyramine, preventing the hypertensive "cheese reaction" seen with non-selective MAOIs.
A 72-year-old man with a history of hypertension and atrial fibrillation presents to the emergency department with sudden onset of right-sided weakness and aphasia that began 45 minutes ago. Non-contrast CT head shows no evidence of hemorrhage. His symptoms progressively worsen over the next 2 hours. Which of the following pathologic mechanisms is most likely responsible for this patient's clinical presentation?
✔ Answer: B
The sudden onset of focal neurological deficits with atrial fibrillation history is most consistent with cardioembolic stroke. The MCA territory is the most common site, and absence of hemorrhage on CT rules out primary hemorrhagic stroke.
Hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy (HIE) in adults most commonly results from global cerebral hypoperfusion due to cardiac arrest, shock, or severe hypotension. Neuropathologically, the hippocampus (particularly CA1 sector, also known as Sommer sector), Purkinje cells of the cerebellum, and pyramidal neurons of layers 3, 5, and 6 of the cerebral cortex are selectively vulnerable. Which of the following best explains this selective vulnerability?
✔ Answer: C
The CA1 hippocampal neurons, Purkinje cells, and cortical pyramidal neurons have exceptionally high metabolic demands due to their constant electrical activity and dependence on aerobic respiration, making them most vulnerable to hypoxic-ischemic injury.
Ischemic stroke results from occlusion of a cerebral artery, leading to a core area of irreversible infarction surrounded by a penumbra of potentially salvageable tissue. The ischemic penumbra is defined by which of the following pathophysiologic characteristics?
✔ Answer: B
The penumbra is defined by blood flow between the threshold for electrical failure (approximately 15-20 mL/100g/min) and the threshold for membrane failure (8-10 mL/100g/min). Cells are functionally silent but structurally intact and potentially salvageable with timely reperfusion.
A 68-year-old woman with a history of hypertension presents with progressive cognitive decline over the past 2 years. Her family reports that she has developed a shuffling gait, urinary incontinence, and emotional lability. Brain MRI reveals extensive white matter hyperintensities in the periventricular and deep white matter regions, as well as multiple lacunar infarcts in the basal ganglia and thalamus. Which of the following is the most likely pathologic diagnosis?
✔ Answer: B
The triad of cognitive impairment, gait disturbance, and urinary incontinence with MRI showing extensive white matter disease and lacunar infarcts is characteristic of Binswanger's disease (subcortical vascular dementia) due to chronic hypertension-induced small vessel disease.
A 55-year-old man presents with a 6-month history of progressive difficulty walking, clumsiness of his hands, and dysarthria. Neurologic examination reveals upper motor neuron signs (hyperreflexia, Babinski sign) in all four limbs as well as lower motor neuron signs (fasciculations, muscle atrophy, weakness) in the bulbar muscles and extremities. There is no sensory loss or ophthalmoplegia. Which of the following neurodegenerative disorders best explains this clinical picture?
✔ Answer: C
The combination of upper motor neuron signs (hyperreflexia, Babinski) and lower motor neuron signs (fasciculations, atrophy, weakness) in the absence of sensory findings is diagnostic of amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS).
Alzheimer's disease is the most common cause of dementia, characterized by progressive cognitive decline, particularly memory impairment. The definitive diagnosis is made at autopsy based on characteristic histopathologic findings. Which of the following correctly pairs the histopathologic finding with its major protein constituent?
✔ Answer: A
Alzheimer's disease has two hallmark lesions: extracellular neuritic (senile) plaques composed of amyloid-beta peptide derived from APP, and intracellular neurofibrillary tangles composed of hyperphosphorylated tau protein.
Parkinson's disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by resting tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, and postural instability. The pathologic hallmark of Parkinson's disease, which correlates with the severity of motor symptoms, is which of the following?
✔ Answer: B
The pathologic hallmark of Parkinson's disease is loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta, accompanied by cytoplasmic inclusions called Lewy bodies (composed of alpha-synuclein).
A 32-year-old man presents with subacute onset of double vision, difficulty walking, and numbness in his left arm. Neurologic examination reveals left internuclear ophthalmoplegia, right-sided weakness, and ataxia. MRI brain shows multiple T2-hyperintense lesions in the periventricular white matter, corpus callosum, and brainstem, with some lesions showing contrast enhancement. Lumbar puncture reveals oligoclonal bands in CSF that are not present in serum. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
✔ Answer: C
The clinical presentation with multiple neurologic deficits separated in space (brainstem, cerebrum) and time (multiple lesions at different stages, some enhancing, some not) with oligoclonal bands is classic for relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis.
A 45-year-old woman presents with acute onset of severe headache, nausea, and photophobia. She describes the headache as "the worst headache of my life" that reached maximal intensity within seconds. Non-contrast CT head shows hyperdense material in the basal cisterns, including the sylvian fissures and interhemispheric fissure. Four-vessel cerebral angiography reveals a saccular outpouching at the junction of the anterior communicating artery and the anterior cerebral artery. Which of the following is the most common complication of this condition?
✔ Answer: A
While all options are potential complications of aneurysmal subarachnoid hemorrhage, cerebral vasospasm (occurring 4-14 days after the bleed) is the most common cause of delayed morbidity and mortality, affecting up to 70% of patients.
Intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) accounts for 10-15% of all strokes but carries a higher mortality rate than ischemic stroke. Chronic hypertension is the most common cause of spontaneous ICH. Which of the following locations is the most common site for hypertensive intracerebral hemorrhage?
✔ Answer: D
Chronic hypertension leads to lipohyalinosis and Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms in small penetrating arteries. The putamen (basal ganglia) is the most common site (35-50%), followed by thalamus, pons, and cerebellum.
Huntington's disease is an autosomal dominant neurodegenerative disorder caused by an expanded CAG trinucleotide repeat in the HTT gene on chromosome 4. The characteristic motor feature is chorea, which is accompanied by cognitive decline and psychiatric disturbances. Which of the following neuropathologic findings is most characteristic of Huntington's disease?
✔ Answer: A
Huntington's disease is characterized by selective degeneration of medium spiny GABAergic neurons in the striatum (caudate and putamen), leading to gross atrophy of the caudate head with secondary dilation of the lateral ventricles ("boxcar ventricles").
A 28-year-old HIV-positive man with a CD4 count of 50 cells/mcL presents with subacute onset of headache, confusion, and focal seizures. Brain MRI shows multiple ring-enhancing lesions in the basal ganglia and subcortical white matter. Serum Toxoplasma gondii IgG antibody is positive. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the characteristic histopathologic finding?
✔ Answer: D
In an AIDS patient with CD4 <100, ring-enhancing lesions with positive Toxoplasma serology are highly suggestive of cerebral toxoplasmosis. Histopathology shows necrotizing abscesses, free tachyzoites, and pseudocysts containing bradyzoites.
Meningitis is an inflammation of the leptomeninges that can be caused by bacteria, viruses, fungi, or other pathogens. Bacterial meningitis is a medical emergency associated with high morbidity and mortality. Which of the following cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) findings is most characteristic of acute bacterial meningitis?
✔ Answer: B
Bacterial meningitis produces a purulent CSF with marked neutrophilic pleocytosis, very low glucose (consumed by bacteria and inflamed cells), and markedly elevated protein (due to increased BBB permeability).
A 65-year-old diabetic man presents with progressive weakness in his right hand over 2 weeks, followed by the development of a painful, vesicular rash in a dermatomal distribution on the right forearm and hand. Neurologic examination reveals weakness and atrophy of the intrinsic hand muscles. Electromyography shows denervation changes in the affected myotomes. Which of the following pathogens is most likely responsible for this condition, and what is the pathologic mechanism?
✔ Answer: B
Herpes zoster (shingles) with segmental motor weakness (zoster paresis) is caused by reactivation of varicella-zoster virus from dorsal root ganglia, with viral spread to anterior horn cells or motor roots.
A 22-year-old woman presents with acute onset of bilateral lower extremity weakness and numbness that began in her feet and has progressed upward over the past 4 days. She reports a diarrheal illness 2 weeks ago. Neurologic examination reveals symmetric flaccid weakness of the lower extremities, areflexia, and mild sensory loss. Nerve conduction studies show slowed conduction velocities and conduction block. Lumbar puncture reveals elevated protein with normal cell count (albuminocytologic dissociation). Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
✔ Answer: B
The acute ascending symmetric weakness with areflexia following an antecedent infection (Campylobacter jejuni), combined with albuminocytologic dissociation on CSF and demyelinating features on nerve conduction studies, is diagnostic of Guillain-Barré syndrome (AIDP).
Myasthenia gravis is an autoimmune disorder affecting the neuromuscular junction. The disease is caused by autoantibodies against which of the following structures, leading to fatigable weakness of extraocular, bulbar, and limb muscles?
✔ Answer: B
In 80-90% of patients with generalized myasthenia gravis, autoantibodies target the nicotinic acetylcholine receptor (AChR) at the postsynaptic membrane, leading to complement-mediated destruction and endplate simplification.
Glioblastoma (WHO grade IV) is the most common and aggressive primary malignant brain tumor in adults. Despite multimodal therapy including surgical resection, radiation, and temozolomide chemotherapy, the prognosis remains poor with median survival of approximately 15 months. Which of the following histopathologic features is characteristic of glioblastoma and distinguishes it from lower-grade astrocytomas?
✔ Answer: B
Glioblastoma is defined histologically by the presence of microvascular proliferation (endothelial hyperplasia) and/or pseudopalisading necrosis (tumor cells aligning around necrotic areas), which are not seen in lower-grade astrocytomas.
A 58-year-old woman presents with progressive hearing loss in her left ear over 6 months, accompanied by tinnitus and episodes of dizziness. MRI with contrast reveals a contrast-enhancing mass lesion at the cerebellopontine angle, extending into the internal auditory canal. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the characteristic histopathologic feature?
✔ Answer: C
Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma) arises from Schwann cells of the vestibular nerve, typically at the cerebellopontine angle. Histopathology shows alternating Antoni A (dense cellular) and Antoni B (loose myxoid) areas with strong S-100 immunopositivity.
A 7-year-old boy presents with morning headaches and vomiting for 3 weeks, followed by progressive difficulty with coordination and truncal ataxia. MRI reveals a midline cerebellar mass that is cystic with an enhancing mural nodule. The mass is surgically resected, and histopathology shows a tumor composed of biphasic tissue with both densely cellular areas and loose microcystic regions, Rosenthal fibers, and eosinophilic granular bodies. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
✔ Answer: C
The presentation (child, cerebellar signs, cystic mass with mural nodule) and histopathology (Rosenthal fibers, eosinophilic granular bodies) are diagnostic of pilocytic astrocytoma (WHO grade I), which has an excellent prognosis with complete surgical resection.
Neurofibromatosis type 1 (NF1, von Recklinghausen disease) is an autosomal dominant neurocutaneous disorder caused by mutations in the NF1 gene encoding neurofibromin. Which of the following pairs of CNS tumors is most commonly associated with neurofibromatosis type 1?
✔ Answer: B
NF1 is associated with optic pathway gliomas (typically pilocytic astrocytomas, seen in 15-20% of children with NF1), cutaneous and plexiform neurofibromas, and other tumors such as malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumors.
A 25-year-old woman presents with acute onset of double vision that is worse at the end of the day and improves with rest. Examination reveals left ptosis that worsens with sustained upward gaze, and the patient develops diplopia when looking to the right that also fatigues. She has no sensory loss or areflexia. Which of the following diagnostic tests is most likely to confirm the diagnosis, and what is the expected finding?
✔ Answer: A
The fatigable weakness with diurnal variation is characteristic of myasthenia gravis. Repetitive nerve stimulation at low frequencies shows a decremental response (>10% decline in amplitude) due to reduced ACh receptor availability.
Peripheral nerve injury is classified by Seddon into three types based on the severity of injury and potential for recovery. Complete transection of the nerve with disruption of the axon, myelin sheath, endoneurium, perineurium, and epineurium, requiring surgical repair for any chance of recovery, is termed:
✔ Answer: C
Neurotmesis is the most severe form of nerve injury with complete anatomical disruption. Recovery is poor without surgical intervention because regenerating axons cannot cross the gap without a guiding conduit.
The McNaughten Rule, established in 1843 following the attempted assassination of British Prime Minister Sir Robert Peel, remains the basis for the insanity defense in many jurisdictions. Under the McNaughten Rule, a defendant is considered legally insane and not criminally responsible if, at the time of the offense, they were laboring under such a defect of reason from disease of the mind that they did not know which of the following?
✔ Answer: B
The McNaughten Rule focuses on cognitive capacity: the defendant must not know the nature and quality of the act, or if they do know it, they must not know that it was wrong according to societal standards.
A 30-year-old man is brought to the emergency department by police after being found wandering on a highway, confused and disoriented. He is unable to provide his name or location. His breath has a strong odor of a solvent-like substance. His pupils are dilated, and he is tachycardic with a blood pressure of 150/95 mmHg. He later becomes agitated and paranoid, believing that people are following him. Which of the following classes of neurotoxins is most likely responsible for this patient's presentation?
✔ Answer: D
The solvent odor on breath, confusion, dilated pupils, tachycardia, hypertension, and agitation are characteristic of inhalant (volatile solvent) intoxication, commonly seen with toluene, gasoline, or paint thinner abuse.
Durham's Rule (also known as the "product test") was established by the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia in 1954 (Durham v. United States). Under this rule, a defendant is not criminally responsible if which of the following conditions is met?
✔ Answer: B
Durham's Rule expanded the insanity defense beyond cognitive incapacity: a defendant is not criminally responsible if the unlawful act was the product of mental disease or defect, regardless of whether they knew right from wrong.
A 45-year-old man with a history of heroin use disorder is found unresponsive in a public restroom with a syringe nearby. Paramedics administer naloxone 2 mg intravenously, and he regains consciousness within 2 minutes. He admits to using heroin approximately 30 minutes before being found. Which of the following pathophysiologic mechanisms explains his initial presentation of respiratory depression and pinpoint pupils?
✔ Answer: A
Heroin (diacetylmorphine) is a mu-opioid receptor agonist. Activation of mu receptors in the brainstem (pre-Bötzinger complex) causes respiratory depression, while activation in the Edinger-Westphal nucleus produces miosis (pinpoint pupils).
Testamentary capacity refers to the mental ability of an individual to make a valid will. Under the legal principles established in Banks v. Goodfellow (1870), a person has testamentary capacity if they can satisfy which of the following criteria?
✔ Answer: A
Testamentary capacity requires: (1) understanding the nature of the act of making a will, (2) knowing the extent of one's property, and (3) comprehending the claims of natural beneficiaries (those who might expect to inherit).
A 28-year-old man is brought to the emergency department after consuming an unknown substance at a party. He is agitated, hyperthermic (40°C), tachycardic (150 bpm), and hypertensive (180/110 mmHg). He reports seeing "colors and patterns" that are not present and expresses fear that "snakes are crawling on the walls." He is also diaphoretic and has mydriasis. Which of the following poisons is most likely responsible for this clinical picture?
✔ Answer: C
Visual hallucinations (colors, patterns), agitation, sympathetic overactivity (hyperthermia, tachycardia, hypertension), and mydriasis are classic features of LSD intoxication, a hallucinogen that acts as a 5-HT2A serotonin receptor agonist.
Forensic psychiatry defines "civil responsibility" as the mental capacity of an individual to manage their own affairs, including entering into contracts, making medical decisions, and managing property. Which of the following conditions would most clearly impair civil responsibility regardless of the presence of cognitive understanding?
✔ Answer: B
Civil responsibility is impaired when a mental disorder directly affects decision-making capacity. Delusional disorders that influence specific decisions (e.g., delusions about finances or medical care) impair civil responsibility even if general cognition is intact.
A 35-year-old man is brought to the hospital after his family found him confused and vomiting. He had reportedly been drinking "homemade alcohol" from an unlabeled bottle. On examination, he is drowsy but arousable, with a respiratory rate of 28 breaths/min and oxygen saturation of 96% on room air. His pupils are dilated and sluggishly reactive. Fundoscopic examination reveals hyperemia and edema of the optic discs. Which of the following is the most likely toxin and the specific finding on fundoscopy?
✔ Answer: B
Methyl alcohol (methanol) is metabolized to formic acid, which causes optic nerve toxicity. Fundoscopy shows hyperemic optic discs initially, followed by optic atrophy and blindness. Methanol is often found in adulterated or homemade alcoholic beverages.
Drug dependence, as defined in forensic medicine and psychiatry, involves both psychological dependence and physical dependence. Physical dependence is characterized by the development of tolerance and the emergence of a withdrawal syndrome upon abrupt discontinuation of the substance. Which of the following classes of drugs produces the most severe and potentially life-threatening withdrawal syndrome, including seizures and delirium tremens?
✔ Answer: B
Withdrawal from sedative-hypnotics (particularly alcohol and benzodiazepines) can cause seizures, autonomic instability, hallucinations (delirium tremens), and death if untreated. Opioid withdrawal, while extremely uncomfortable, is not life-threatening.
A 22-year-old woman is brought to the psychiatric emergency department by her friends. She reports that she took "a few pills" from her grandmother's medicine cabinet approximately 4 hours ago. She is drowsy but arousable, with slurred speech, ataxia, and nystagmus on horizontal gaze. Vital signs show mild hypotension (90/60 mmHg) and bradycardia (52 bpm). Which of the following classes of drugs is most likely responsible for this presentation, and what is the characteristic pupillary finding?
✔ Answer: B
Barbiturate toxicity produces CNS depression with drowsiness, ataxia, nystagmus, slurred speech, and respiratory depression. Pupils are typically normal to slightly constricted (unlike opioids which cause marked miosis).
The Mental Health Act in most jurisdictions provides for the involuntary admission and treatment of individuals with mental disorders who pose a danger to themselves or others. Which of the following criteria is universally required for involuntary psychiatric hospitalization?
✔ Answer: B
Involuntary hospitalization requires: (1) presence of a mental disorder, (2) danger to self (suicidality, inability to provide basic needs) OR danger to others (violence, threatening behavior), and (3) lack of capacity to consent to voluntary treatment.
A 19-year-old university student is brought to the emergency department after collapsing during a party. Friends report that he had been drinking alcohol and also took "a small amount of cocaine" earlier in the evening. He is now unresponsive with a respiratory rate of 6 breaths/min, heart rate of 48 bpm, and blood pressure of 70/40 mmHg. His pupils are pinpoint. Which of the following drug interactions best explains his severe respiratory depression?

Pyogenic meningitis in children is a medical emergency requiring prompt recognition and treatment. The most common bacterial pathogens vary by age. In a previously healthy 6-month-old infant, which of the following is the most common causative organism of bacterial meningitis?
✔ Answer: C
Before widespread Hib vaccination, Haemophilus influenzae type b was the most common cause of bacterial meningitis in children aged 2 months to 5 years. Since vaccination, S. pneumoniae and N. meningitidis have become more prevalent.
A 55-year-old woman with a history of hypertension and diabetes is scheduled for elective total knee replacement surgery. During the pre-anesthetic evaluation, she reports that she takes metformin, lisinopril, and atorvastatin. Her last meal was 3 hours ago. Which of the following is the most appropriate recommendation regarding her preoperative management?
✔ Answer: E
Metformin should be withheld on the day of surgery and for 24-48 hours after surgery, especially if there is risk of renal impairment, contrast use, or hypotension, to prevent metformin-associated lactic acidosis. Clear liquid intake up to 2 hours before surgery is generally acceptable.
Spinal anesthesia (subarachnoid block) involves injection of local anesthetic into the cerebrospinal fluid within the subarachnoid space, typically at the L3-L4 or L4-L5 interspace. Which of the following is an absolute contraindication to performing spinal anesthesia?
✔ Answer: C
Therapeutic anticoagulation is an absolute contraindication to neuraxial anesthesia (spinal or epidural) due to the risk of spinal epidural hematoma, which can cause permanent paraplegia. Antiplatelet agents (aspirin alone) are not absolute contraindications.
A 25-year-old medical student is bitten on the right hand by a stray dog while volunteering at an animal shelter in a rabies-endemic area. The wound is superficial but has broken the skin with minimal bleeding. The dog cannot be located for observation. The student has never received rabies vaccination previously. Which of the following represents the complete post-exposure rabies prophylaxis regimen according to WHO guidelines?
✔ Answer: C
Category III exposure (any transdermal bite in a rabies-endemic area from an unavailable animal) requires immediate wound cleansing, rabies immunoglobulin (RIG) infiltrated into the wound, and a full course of rabies vaccine (4 doses).
Peripheral nerve injuries are classified by Seddon into three types: neurapraxia, axonotmesis, and neurotmesis. The prognosis for recovery differs significantly among these types. Which of the following correctly describes axonotmesis?
✔ Answer: C
Axonotmesis involves disruption of the axon but preservation of the endoneurial sheath. Wallerian degeneration occurs distally, but axons can regenerate along the intact endoneurial tubes at a rate of approximately 1 mm/day, with variable functional recovery.
A 28-year-old construction worker falls from a height of 15 feet, landing on his feet. He is unable to move his legs and reports loss of sensation below the waist. In the emergency department, his blood pressure is 85/50 mmHg and heart rate is 55 bpm. He has flaccid paralysis of both lower extremities, areflexia, and no rectal tone. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the pathophysiology of his hypotension and bradycardia?
✔ Answer: C
Neurogenic shock occurs with cervical or high thoracic (T1-T6) spinal cord injury due to interruption of sympathetic outflow from the spinal cord, resulting in hypotension (unopposed vasodilation) and bradycardia (unopposed vagal tone).
A 35-year-old man sustains a deep laceration to his volar forearm from a broken glass bottle. He is unable to flex the distal interphalangeal joint of his left ring finger. Which of the following nerves and muscles are most likely injured?
✔ Answer: B
The ulnar nerve innervates the flexor digitorum profundus to the ring and little fingers (via the ulnar head). Injury to the ulnar nerve at the forearm level results in inability to flex the DIP joints of the ring and little fingers.
Spinal tumors can be classified based on their anatomical location relative to the spinal cord and dura. Which of the following is the most common primary intradural, extramedullary spinal tumor in adults?
✔ Answer: E
Schwannomas (derived from Schwann cells of nerve roots) are the most common intradural extramedullary spinal tumors. Meningiomas are the second most common. Ependymomas and astrocytomas are intramedullary (within the cord substance).
Preoperative risk assessment using the American Society of Anesthesiologists (ASA) Physical Status Classification System is essential for perioperative planning. Which of the following patients would be classified as ASA III?
✔ Answer: C
ASA III indicates a patient with severe systemic disease that limits activity but is not incapacitating (e.g., stable angina, COPD, diabetes with end-organ damage). ASA I = healthy; ASA II = mild systemic disease; ASA IV = incapacitating disease; ASA V = moribund.
A 10-year-old boy is brought to the emergency department by his mother after he developed a fever (39.5°C), severe headache, neck stiffness, and photophobia over the past 12 hours. On examination, he is lethargic but arousable. Kernig's sign and Brudzinski's sign are positive. Lumbar puncture shows 2000 white blood cells/mcL (95% neutrophils), glucose of 20 mg/dL (serum glucose 100 mg/dL), and protein of 250 mg/dL. Gram stain shows Gram-positive cocci in pairs. Which of the following is the most appropriate initial empiric antibiotic regimen?
✔ Answer: B
The CSF findings are consistent with bacterial meningitis. Gram-positive cocci in pairs suggest Streptococcus pneumoniae. Empiric therapy for suspected pneumococcal meningitis requires vancomycin plus a third-generation cephalosporin (ceftriaxone or cefotaxime) due to emerging penicillin and cephalosporin resistance.
